## ABSTRACT

Trajectory similarity computation is a fundamental problem in various applications (e.g., transportation optimization, behavioral study). Recent researches learn trajectory representations instead of point matching to realize more accurate and efficient trajectory similarity computation. However, these methods can still not be scaled to large datasets due to high computational cost. In this paper, we propose a novel hash learning method to encode the trajectories into binary hash codes and compute trajectory similarities by Hamming distances which is much more efficient. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to conduct hash learning for trajectory similarity computation. Furthermore, unlike the Word2Vec model based on random walk strategy, we utilize hypergraph neural networks for the first time to learn the representations for the grids by constructing the hyperedges according to the real-life trajectories, resulting in more representative grid embeddings. In addition, we design a residual network into the multi-layer GRU to learn more discriminative trajectory representations. The proposed Hypergraph Hash Learning for Trajectory similarity commutation is an end-to-end framework and named HHL-Traj. Experimental results on two real-world trajectory datasets (i.e., Porto and Beijing) demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves up to 6.23% and 15.42% accuracy gains compared with state-of-the-art baselines in unhashed and hashed cases, respectively. The efficiency of trajectory similarity computation based on hash codes is also verified. Our code is available at https://github.com/caoyuan57/HHL-Traj.

# 1 INTRODUCTION

With the proliferation of GPS-enable devices and the rapid development of wireless communication technology, massive trajectory data has been collected at an unprecedented speed, which describes the motion of an object over time with a discrete spatial-temporal sequence. Trajectory similarity computation is a fundamental problem in a variety of trajectory analytic tasks, such as trajectory representation learning \([8, 12, 42, 44]\) , trajectory matching \([85]\) , trajectory prediction \([48, 51, 59, 82, 90]\) , trajectory recovery \([10, 45, 62, 67]\) , and trajectory clustering \([34, 39, 65, 72]\) .

Traditional heuristic metrics, such as dynamic time wrapping (DTW) [89], Hausdorff distance[2], Fréchet distance [1], longest common subsequence (LCSS) [71], edit distance with the real penalty (ERP) [5], and edit distance on real sequences (EDR) [7], edit distance with projections (EDwP) [61], adopt pairwise matching, compute the optimal alignment by dynamic programming, and count the distances between two aligned trajectories, which results in quadratic computational complexity \(O(n^2)\) , where \(n\) denotes the sample number in the trajectories. Moreover, the performance of these metrics may be degraded when the sample points are noisy, which may occur in GPS when moving in urban canyons [46].

Therefore, recent studies [11, 43, 73, 75, 86] leverage deep networks to project the trajectories into latent space and learn their

deep embeddings, while preserving similarities between the trajectories. Specifically, one category [88] aims at minimizing the difference between the similarities in the latent space and ground-truth (mentioned above). Due to the lack of ground-truth for trajectory similarity, the other category [37] focuses on constructing positive and negative trajectory pairs and uses self-supervised contrastive learning for representation learning. These methods are capable of automatic representation learning and tackling different data distributions with complex patterns. Furthermore, the trajectory similarity can be computed in linear time by evaluating similarities between the vectors. However, as the scale of trajectory data increases rapidly, linear computational complexity is still not satisfactory in applications that require real-time feedback.

According to different mapping ways for trajectory point data, trajectory similarity computation methods can be divided into road network based methods \([8, 36, 37, 78]\) and grid based methods \([9, 38, 95]\) . Road network is based on a road system consisting of various interconnected and interwoven roads. Road network representation learning encodes the roads into vectors containing the graph topology and intersection information \([57]\) . Recent researches utilize graph-based models, such as graph neural networks (GNN) \([63]\) and graph attention networks (GAT) \([70]\) , to learn road representations, followed by sequential networks to learn trajectory embeddings. This kind of method leverages the topology and attribute information of road networks to accurately capture the movement patterns and behavioral characteristics of trajectory data, thereby enhancing the accuracy of trajectory similarity calculation. However, as road networks become increasingly large and complex, the complexity of their graph structures also increases. Handing the large number of nodes and edges in the graph structure produces high computational and storage complexity and more training time.

In contrast, grid-based methods map trajectory data into a grid space and learn grid representations based on word embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec) and random walk strategy \([58]\) . Then, sequential networks \([94]\) are subsequently used to learn trajectory data representations. These methods simplify the data processing procedure and can deal with various types of trajectory data. Nevertheless, since gird representations are learnt by using fake trajectories produced by random walk strategy rather than real-life trajectories, this kind of method cannot capture spatial patterns and behavioral characteristics of real-life trajectory data, which limits the accuracy of trajectory similarity calculation.

To address the aforementioned issues, we proposed a novel Hypergraph Hash Learning framework for efficient Trajectory similarity computation (HHL-Traj), which is a deep embedding based and grid based approach. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first hash learning work for the trajectory similarity computation task. We first map trajectory data into a grid space and construct a hypergraph based on real-life trajectories. To be specific, the nodes of the hypergraph are initialized by random grid representations, and the hyperedges are built by regarding the trajectory points in real-life trajectories. In addition, we introduce residual connections into our hypergraph neural networks for better representation learning. In comparison with grid based methods, since we avoid the pitfalls of deviating from real-life trajectories, our method allows for high-quality initialization processing of grid representations and enhances the accuracy of following trajectory similarity calculation.

In contrast to road network based methods, we eliminate the need to handle the topology information of road networks and can also obtain satisfactory representation initialization. Furthermore, our model reduces the computational and storage costs by replacing the complex graph structure with hyperedges.

As for trajectory representation learning, we design a residual network into the multi-layer GRU to encode the grid sequence representations into trajectory representations. Furthermore, we add a fully-connected hash layer to get their binary hash codes. It is worth noticing that the hash layer is a plug-and-play layer. In trajectory similarity search applications, Hamming distances between hash codes can be calculated to return a candidate set containing similar trajectories to the query and re-ranking can be realized by real-value trajectory similarity computations in the candidate set, which balances both efficiency and accuracy. Finally, we construct self-supervised contrastive learning loss to preserve similarities and quantization loss for binarization. In order to design the positive pairs, we conduct an augmentation technique by a dropout rate of 0.5 on each trajectory twice. The other masked subtrajectories are considered as the negative examples. The contrastive learning loss is constructed to make positive trajectory pairs close and negative trajectory pairs far away.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as:

- We utilize hypergraph neural networks with residual connections to embed the grids based on real-life trajectory data and design a residual network in the multi-layer GRU to get more discriminative trajectory representations.  
- We propose a novel hash learning framework based on contrastive learning loss and quantization loss to balance efficiency and accuracy. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to realize hash learning for trajectory similarity computation.  
- We conduct experiments on two commonly used trajectory datasets, i.e., Porto and Beijing. The experimental results show that the proposed framework obtains up to 6.23% and 15.42% accuracy gains compared with state-of-the-art baselines in unhashed and hashed cases, respectively. Moreover, trajectory similarity calculation with hash codes performs 4.6 and 5.7 times faster than that with real-value representations on Beijing and Porto, respectively.

Then, we give preliminary in Section 3 and describe the proposed method in Section 4. Next, we conduct experiments and analyse the results in Section 5. Finally, we conclude this paper in Section 6.

# 2 RELATED WORK

## 2.1 Trajectory Similarity Calculation

Trajectory similarity computation is the key component in a variety of trajectory analytic tasks, which can be categorized into traditional heuristic metrics \([1, 2, 7, 61, 71, 89]\) and deep embedding based metrics \([9, 95]\) . In the former, pairwise matching between two trajectories is adopted and the optimal alignment is computed by dynamic programming. Then, a distance function is used to measure pairwise trajectory similarity. For example, dynamic time

warping (DTW) [89] computes the distance between corresponding positions of points in the trajectories. Hausdorff [2] computes the maximum point-to-trajectory distance between two trajectories. Fréchet [1] resembles Hausdorff distance and requires that the point matching strictly follows the sequential trajectory point order. Longest common subsequence (LCSS) [71] provides an intuitive notion of similarity between trajectories by giving more weight to similar portions of the sequences. Edit distance with the real penalty (ERP) [6] conducts a marriage of Manhattan distance and the edit distance which supports local time shifting. EDR [7] and EDwP [61] both compute the edit distance between trajectories, while EDwP further considers the real point distances by computing the cheapest set of replacement and insertion operations using linear interpolation [4]. However, due to high computational and storage complexity, traditional heuristic metrics have mostly been replaced by deep embedding based metrics recently, which aim at embedding trajectories into latent vectors with deep learning models. Deep embedding based metrics can realize trajectory similarity computation by evaluating distances between two vectors in linear time and meanwhile show excellent performance on downstream tasks.

One stream of research learns trajectory representations based on road networks. Trembr [19] initializes real-life road topology structure using Word2Vec model and integrates it into t2vec model [46] to learn trajectory representations with an encoder-decoder module for training. GTS [30] constructs a graph structure with both POI points and road networks. It combines GNN networks [64] and LSTM networks [32] to obtain trajectory features for similarity calculation. GRLSTM [97] designs the road network as a graph structure using knowledge graph. Nodes are constructed by road segments and edges indicate road intersections between two segments. Then, GAT networks [70] and sequential networks are utilized to extract trajectory features for similarity calculation. However, the graph structure in these methods is complex due to the intricate road network, resulting in high training costs.

The other stream of research learns trajectory representations based on grids. t2vec [46] is the first deep learning model applied to trajectory similarity calculation. It utilizes a pre-training algorithm and an RNN based model [77] to learn trajectory representations. A spatial proximity perception loss function is designed to encourage the consistency of similar trajectory representations. NeuTraj [87] utilizes LSTM networks [32] and spatial memory modules to capture the correlations between trajectory representations. TrajGAT [88] proposes a neural network based on transformer model [69] to capture long-term dependencies between trajectories. It constructs a quadtree to balance the number of trajectory points within nodes in the road network. By training the transformer network with quadtree information, it alleviates the high training cost problem produced by large amount of parameters in transformer networks. However, random strategies are inevitably employed during grid representation initialization, which ignores the connection relationships between the grids in the real world and limits the performance of trajectory similarity computation.

## 2.2 Graph Neural Networks

Graph neural networks (GNNs) [64] is a class of models that encode graph-structured data into low-dimensional vectors for subsequent learning and processing. Graph convolutional networks (GCNs) [41] typically consist of multiple graph convolutional layers, each of which updates node representations to capture the global structure of the graph through aggregation. Graph attention networks (GAT) [70] utilize attention mechanisms to learn the relationships between the nodes. Each node dynamically assigns different weights to its neighboring nodes during the aggregation process, thereby capturing better information in the graph structure. Hypergraph neural networks (HGNNs) [18] is designed for hypergraph data. In contrast to GNNs, hyperedges can be constructed among any number of nodes. By performing information aggregation and propagation on the hypergraph, node features are updated. In this paper, we for the first time utilize HGNNs to obtain grid representations for better trajectory similarity computation.

## 2.3 Deep Hash Learning

Learning to Hash [25–27, 54] has been widely used in computer vision applications, such as image retrieval [49, 55, 80], image locating [29, 79, 83], multi-modal information processing [53, 96, 98], etc, because of its high computational efficiency. Hashing is to encode high-dimensional real vectors into low-dimensional binary codes, while preserving similarities. Locality sensitive hashing (LSH) [21] is the first representative hashing method by adopting data-independent random hash functions that follow a Gaussian distribution. With the rapid development of deep learning[13, 20, 22–24, 28, 52], a large number of deep hashing methods are proposed and can be divided into supervised ones [50, 60, 81, 93] and unsupervised ones [15, 55, 56, 68, 84], according to whether label ground-truth information is utilized for training. As for unsupervised deep hashing methods, the loss functions are designed to preserve similarities based on the original space [33, 47, 54, 84] or in a self-supervised way [3, 35, 66, 74, 76] (including contrastive learning [35, 40, 74]). Although Driemel et al. [16] apply LSH [21] to Fréchet and Hausdorff distances for fast computing, we for the first time conduct hash learning on trajectory data for efficient trajectory similarity computation.

## 3.1 Definitions

Definition 3.1 (Trajectory). A trajectory \((\tau)\) of a moving object is a finite sequence of points sampled from the underlining route with the form of \(\tau = ((x_1, y_1, t_1), ..., (x_i, y_i, t_i), ..., (x_n, y_n, t_n))\) , where \((x_i, y_i, t_i)\) denotes the longitude and latitude information of a sampled location at timestamp \(t_i^1\) .

Definition 3.2 (Grid). The whole space containing all the trajectories is partitioned into non-overlapping and adjacent grids with equal size and each grid is treated as a token. All the latitude-longitude points in the same grid will be mapped to the same token.

## 3.2 Problem Definition

Given a trajectory set \(T = \{\tau_{1}, ..., \tau_{j}, ..., \tau_{m}\}\) , for \(\forall \tau_{a} \in T\) , trajectory similarity computation is to find a trajectory \(\tau_{b} \in T\) such that \(\tau_{b}\) is most similar to \(\tau_{a}\) , where \(a \neq b\) .

## 4.2 Grid Division Module

Similar to previous grid based trajectory similarity calculation models [46, 87, 88], we partition the latitude and longitude space into non-overlapping and adjacent grids with equal size as shown in Figure 2. The grids are treated as tokens and denoted as \(\{g_1, g_2, ..., g_N\}\) , where \(N\) denotes the total number of the grids. All the latitude-longitude points falling into the same grid are mapped to the same token. After all the trajectory points in a trajectory are represented by the grid tokens, we obtain a series of representations for the trajectory. For example, as for a trajectory \(\tau_j = ((x_1, y_1), ..., (x_i, y_i), ...,\)

\((x_{n},y_{n}))\) , if \((x_{i},y_{i})\) falls into the grid \(g_{ji}\) , we get the grid sequence corresponding to \(\tau_{j}\) as \((g_{j1},...,g_{jk},...,g_{jK_{j}})\) , where \(K_{j}\) denotes the total number of the grids \(\tau_{j}\) passes.

## 4.3 Residual Hypergraph Networks

In previous grid based trajectory similarity calculation methods, the grid representations are initialized by a word embedding model with the random walk strategy. However, this preprocessing has two main drawbacks. First, it overlooks the real-life connections between the grids. As shown in Figure 2, there is no trajectory that passes grid 15 and 16 simultaneously. However, a fake trajectory that passes grid 15 and 16 may be generated based on the random walk strategy, which is perceived as an obvious error example. Nevertheless, the word embedding model still takes the erroneous connections into consideration, resulting in poor grid representations. Second, in previous methods, grid representations initialization and trajectory representations learning are based on two independent models. Therefore, the word embedding pre-training model does not participate in training to capture real-life spatial information.

In our approach, we randomly initialize the grid representations \(X = (g_{1}, g_{2}, ..., g_{N})\) and utilize hypergraph neural networks to update them. In preparation, we conduct augmentation on each trajectory \(\tau_{j}\) by a dropout rate of 0.5 on \(\tau_{j}\) twice to get two subtrajectories \(\tau_{j}^{(1)}\) and \(\tau_{j}^{(2)}\) and corresponding grid sequences \((g_{1}^{(1)}, g_{2}^{(1)}, ..., g_{K_{j}}^{(1)})\) and \((g_{1}^{(2)}, g_{2}^{(2)}, ..., g_{K_{j}}^{(2)})\) . Now, we construct a hypergraph structure \(<\mathcal{G} = (\mathcal{V}, \mathcal{E}) >\) , where V is defined by the grid representations \((g_{1}, g_{2}, ..., g_{N})\) and E is defined according to the real-life subtrajectory sequences, i.e.,

\[
\mathcal {V} = \left\{v _ {i} = g _ {i} \mid i = 1, \dots , N \right\}, \tag {1}
\]

\[
\mathcal {E} = \left\{e _ {j} \mid e _ {j} \text {joints} \left\{g _ {j 1} ^ {(s)},..., g _ {j _ {K _ {j}}} ^ {(s)} \right\} j = 1,..., 2 m, s = 1, 2 \right\}, \tag {2}
\]

where 2m denotes the number of subtrajectories. Consequently, we obtain the adjacency matrix \(H \in R^{N \times 2m}\) of the hypergraph network as

\[
H _ {i j} = \left\{ \begin{array}{l} 1, i f \tau_ {j} ^ {(s)} \text {passes} g _ {i}, \\ 0, o t h e r w i s e, \end{array} \right. \tag {3}
\]

where \(g_{i}\) denotes the i-th grid token. Subsequently, we derive the corresponding node degree vector \(D_{v}\) and edge degree vector \(D_{e}\) based on the adjacency matrix H as

\[
D _ {v i} = \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {2 m} H _ {i j}, \tag {4}
\]

\[
D _ {e j} = \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {N} H _ {i j}, \tag {5}
\]

where \(D_{vi}\) and \(D_{ej}\) denote the \(i\) -element in \(D_v\) and \(j\) -th element in \(D_e\) , respectively.

Now, we update the grid representations X using hypergraph convolution operations [18]:

\[
X ^ {(l + 1)} = \sigma (D _ {v} ^ {- 1 / 2} H W D _ {e} ^ {- 1} D _ {v} ^ {- 1 / 2} X ^ {(l)} \Theta^ {(l)}), \tag {6}
\]

where \(l\) denotes the convolution layer, \(W \in \mathbb{R}^{2m \times 2m}\) denotes the weights of the edges, \(\Theta\) denotes the parameters in the fully-connected layers and \(\sigma\) denotes the Relu activation function. The updated \(X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}\) denotes the updated grid representations, where \(d\) denotes the dimensionality.

Inspired by the ResNet model \([31]\) , we introduce the residual idea into the hypergraph neural networks. We add residual connections to each hypergraph layer. Hence, a combination of the output of the previous layer and the current layer is input into the next hypergraph layer as

\[
X ^ {(l + 1)} = X ^ {(l + 1)} + X ^ {(l)}. \tag {7}
\]

During the updating process of the grid representations, while they are updated by considering the other nodes, they can also retain their unique information, which helps obtain superior grid representations.

By observing (6), we find that during the updating process of the hypergraph neural networks, the edges first aggregate their connected vertices' features and combine them to generate temporary edge features. Subsequently, the vertices update their own features based on the set of edge features connected to them. Correspondingly, when updating grid representations, the related grid representations based on real-life trajectory sequences are taken into consideration, thereby producing superior representations than those based on random walk models. Moreover, our grid representation learning networks can be trained together with subsequent sequence networks, realizing end-to-end learning.

On the other hand, in comparison with graph neural networks like GNNs and GCNs, HGNNs tend to have smoother node feature transitions during training, because in normal graph neural networks, node features are updated only based on a small number of adjacent nodes. However, in HGNNs, due to the presence of the hyperedges, each node owns more adjacent nodes, which fastens the smoothing rate of the node features.

## 4.4 Residual GRU Networks

Previous researches on trajectory similarity calculation tasks utilize recurrent neural networks (RNNs) \([77]\) to learn trajectory representations, because trajectory data is a typical type of sequential data. In our method, we adopt gated recurrent unit (GRU) networks for trajectory representation learning. Compared with traditional RNNs, GRU adds a forgetting mechanism, allowing it to determine the importance of the current node and its impact ratio. Compared with LSTM networks \([32]\) , GRU has a simpler internal structure and faster training speed. Compared with transformer networks \([69]\) , GRU consumes fewer memory resources and can be trained faster. Therefore, GRU is an appropriate choice for trajectory representation learning.

We first construct the grid representation sequences Y corresponding to the trajectory points order in the subtrajectories \(\{\tau_{j}^{(s)}, j = 1, ..., m; s = 1, 2\}\) . Then, we input Y into the GRU networks to learn their subtrajectory representations as

\[
Z = G R U (Y). \tag {8}
\]

Inspired by GRLSTM networks [97], we also introduce residual networks into the multi-layer GRU networks to solve the gradient

Vanishing problem in multi-layer GRU networks, which is formulated as

\[
Z ^ {(l + 1)} = \sigma (Z) + Z ^ {(l)}. \tag {9}
\]

## 4.5 Hash Learning Network

We build a deep hash network by adding a fully-connected layer behind GRU. The deep hash network can encode input data into binary hash codes, which is widely used for image data processing and has been proven to be efficient on large-scale datasets. Here, the trajectory representations Z output by GRU are input into our hash network to get their hash codes:

\[
B = \operatorname{sgn} (H) = \operatorname{sgn} (\tanh (F (Z; \theta))), \tag {10}
\]

where H denotes the continuous values before quantization \(((-1,1))\) and B denotes the binary codes \((\{-1,1\})\) , \(\theta\) denotes the parameters in the hash network, tanh function is used to map the values to the range \((-1,1)\) , which is normal in hashing, sgn denotes the piecewise function which is defined as

\[
\operatorname{sgn} (x) = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} 1 & x > 0, \\ - 1 & \text {otherwise.} \end{array} \right. \tag {11}
\]

As for a trajectory \(\tau\) , we denote its binary code as

\[
b = \operatorname{sgn} (h) = \operatorname{sgn} (\tanh (F (\tau ; \theta))). \tag {12}
\]

Hash codes contain only two values (-1 and 1), which are more compact than the original real-value representations. Therefore, storage cost can be significantly reduced. Additionally, since the Hamming distance between hash codes can be calculated through bit-wise operations, efficient trajectory similarity computation based on hash codes can be achieved.

## 4.6 Objective Function

In order to address the challenge of lacking ground-truth for trajectory datasets, we opt for using info noise contrastive estimation (InfoNCE) based contrastive learning loss which is defined as

\[
\mathcal {L} _ {c} = \sum_ {p = 1} ^ {2 m} - \log \frac {\exp (S _ {p 1 2} ^ {+})}{\sum_ {q = 1} ^ {m} \sum_ {s = 1} ^ {2} \exp (S _ {p q} ^ {(s)})}, \tag {13}
\]

where \(S_{p12}^{+}=cos(h_{p}^{(1)},h_{p}^{(2)})\) denotes the cosine similarity between two positive subtrajectory pairs \(\tau_{p}^{(1)}\) and \(\tau_{p}^{(2)}\) , \(S_{pq}^{(s)}=cos(h_{p}^{(s)},h_{q}^{(s)})\) denotes the cosine similarity between two negative trajectory pairs \(\tau_{p}^{(s)}\) and \(\tau_{q}^{(s)}\) , where cosine similarity \(\cos(x,y)\) is defined as

\[
\cos (x, y) = \frac {x ^ {\mathrm{T}} y}{\| x \| \| y \|}, \tag {14}
\]

where \(\|\cdot\|\) denotes the \(\ell_{2}\) -norm. As shown in (13), our framework considers both the consistency of trajectory representations between similar trajectories and the separation between dissimilar trajectories.

Finally, we present the quantization loss function in our method for binarization:

\[
\mathcal {L} _ {q} = \sum_ {p = 1} ^ {2 m} \left(1 - \left| h _ {p} \right|\right). \tag {15}
\]

The overall objective function can be formulated as

\[
\mathcal {L} = \mathcal {L} _ {c} + \alpha \mathcal {L} _ {q}, \tag {16}
\]

where \(\alpha\) denotes a hyperparameter for balance.

# 6 CONCLUSION

In this paper, we propose a novel hypergraph hash learning framework for trajectory similarity computation. We introduce residual connections into hypergraph neural networks and apply them to update the grid representations. Furthermore, we design residual networks into the multi-layer GRU for trajectory representations learning. In the end, we construct a plug-and-play hash network to get their hash codes. This is the first work to apply hypergraph neural networks to the trajectory similarity computation task and also the first work to realize hash learning on trajectory data. Experimental results demonstrate the accuracy and efficiency of the proposed method.
